FINTRUST AI: ENTERPRISE RISK AND COMPLIANCE COPILOT
Detailed product and solution proposal for review by seniors, professors, mentors, domain experts, and potential stakeholders

Prepared for: product/technical validation and implementation planning
Target direction: Modern AI product prototype for financial services support, compliance, risk, and document intelligence
Suggested duration for MVP: 6 to 8 weeks with disciplined execution
Suggested positioning: Startup-style AI/RAG solution for source-grounded financial-services knowledge work


1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Project name:
FinTrust AI: Enterprise Risk and Compliance Copilot

One-line description:
FinTrust AI is a live AI application that helps a financial-services support or compliance team answer policy, customer-service, credit-card, KYC, grievance, and regulatory questions using trusted documents, citations, risk labels, and audit logs.

Simple explanation:
Instead of building a generic "PDF chatbot", we will build a serious document-intelligence system for a corporate finance use case. A user can ask questions such as:

- What should a customer do if a credit card complaint is not resolved within 30 days?
- Which document clause supports this answer?
- Is this complaint related to card fraud, grievance redressal, KYC, fees, chargeback, or service deficiency?
- Can the system draft a safe support response for a human agent?
- Is the generated answer fully grounded in retrieved sources?

The system will answer only from trusted documents, show citations, show retrieved source chunks, classify the case, flag risk, and store an audit trail.

The main idea:
Companies do not want AI systems that only "sound smart." In finance, support, compliance, and risk teams need AI systems that are traceable, explainable, measurable, and safe. FinTrust AI is designed as a focused product prototype for that modern problem.

This project was chosen because it sits at the intersection of:

1. Real enterprise pain point
2. Strong need for trustworthy AI in regulated workflows
3. Practical public data availability
4. Achievable 1-2 month MVP scope
5. Startup/product potential

The problem is not a lack of chatbots. The problem is that most AI assistants are not reliable enough for document-heavy financial workflows where answers need sources, auditability, and human review. FinTrust AI is chosen because it addresses this gap directly.

Why not choose a generic project?

Generic options like "basic chatbot", "movie recommender", "fake news detector", or "stock price predictor" are common. They can still be useful for learning, but they often do not solve an urgent operational problem unless executed at a very high level.

FinTrust AI is stronger because it is:

- Corporate-relevant
- AI/ML + backend + product combined
- Useful in regulated industries
- Easy to explain to non-technical seniors
- Deep enough for technical/product review
- Possible to make live within 6-8 weeks
- Connected to real business workflows like customer support, compliance review, risk analysis, policy QA, and audit documentation

Honest answer:
This is genuinely needed as a type of solution, but the first version should be treated as an MVP/prototype, not a production system ready for a bank.

Why the need is real:

Financial institutions, banks, fintech companies, insurance companies, card issuers, and enterprise compliance teams deal with many documents:

- Regulatory guidelines
- Internal policies
- Customer complaint rules
- Credit card terms
- KYC/AML policies
- Product documentation
- Audit notes
- Support knowledge bases
- Customer complaint histories
- Case resolution procedures

Employees often need to search through long documents to answer questions. A wrong answer can create customer dissatisfaction, compliance risk, audit issues, or regulatory problems. Because of this, companies are interested in AI systems that can retrieve trusted information, generate answers, show citations, and keep audit logs.

Research support:

- Stripe compliance-related agentic AI discussions emphasize human oversight, transparency, audit trails, and documented actions.

- American Express AI roles mention production-grade RAG over sensitive financial data, agentic workflows, evaluation, security, reliability, auditability, and compliance.
- Public banking/customer complaint datasets and RBI public documents make a realistic prototype possible without using private data.

Why the prototype is still valuable:

We will not claim that the first prototype is ready for real customer deployment. That would be dishonest. Instead, we will say:

A stronger product-facing version of the same statement is:

"This MVP demonstrates a source-grounded financial document intelligence workflow for support, risk, and compliance teams, with citations, risk labels, human review, and audit logging."

That is a credible and impressive claim.

Where it has real importance:

- Compliance teams can use similar systems to review rules and prepare answers.

Where we must be careful:

4. EXACT PROBLEM STATEMENT

Problem:
Financial service teams spend time searching long policy and regulatory documents to answer customer support, compliance, and grievance questions. Generic LLMs can produce fluent but unsupported answers. In regulated environments, unsupported answers are risky because teams need source-backed, auditable, and consistent responses.

Proposed solution:
Build a web application where a user can ask finance-support or compliance questions. The system retrieves relevant source passages from trusted public documents, generates a grounded answer, displays citations, classifies the query type, flags risk level, drafts a human-reviewable response, and stores an audit trail of the interaction.

Target users for prototype:

- Customer support analyst
- Compliance associate
- Risk operations analyst
- Founder or builder exploring financial AI systems
- Professor/mentor reviewing applied GenAI project
- Product/technical stakeholder reviewing feasibility

Example user journeys:

1. Compliance question:
User asks: "If a credit card issuer does not resolve a customer complaint within 30 days, what can the customer do?"
System retrieves relevant RBI Ombudsman and credit card grievance rules, answers with citations, and says this is a grievance-redressal issue.

2. Support ticket classification:
User pastes: "My credit card was charged an annual fee that was not disclosed and support has not replied for 40 days."
System classifies it as "fees + grievance redressal", gives a risk level, retrieves relevant clauses, and drafts a polite response for a human agent.

3. KYC/identity query:
User asks: "Why is a bank asking for updated KYC documents?"
System retrieves KYC-related document chunks and explains in simple language with citation.

4. Unsupported question:
User asks: "Which bank will approve my loan?"
System should refuse or say insufficient source context, because the documents do not support that answer.


5. WHAT EXACTLY THE PROJECT WILL SOLVE

The project solves these practical sub-problems:

1. Document search problem
Long financial/regulatory documents are hard to search manually. RAG helps retrieve relevant paragraphs based on semantic meaning, not only keyword matching.

2. Hallucination problem
LLMs can invent answers. RAG reduces this risk by grounding answers in retrieved documents. Citations and source chunks make the answer checkable.

3. Support consistency problem
Different agents may answer the same policy question differently. A grounded assistant can provide more consistent draft responses.

4. Complaint classification problem
Customer complaints need routing: fraud, KYC, refund, card issue, grievance, fees, chargeback, transfer issue, etc. A classifier can help prioritize and route.

5. Auditability problem
Finance and compliance teams need to know: who asked what, which sources were used, what answer was generated, and when. Audit logs address this.

6. Evaluation problem
Many AI demos only "look good." This project will measure quality using a test set and RAG metrics such as answer relevance, faithfulness, context precision, and latency.


6. PROJECT SCOPE

Recommended final scope for 6-8 weeks:

Core modules:

1. Document ingestion
Upload or load PDFs, web pages, or text documents.

2. Text extraction and cleaning
Extract readable content from PDFs and HTML sources.

3. Chunking
Split documents into meaningful chunks with metadata.

4. Embeddings
Convert chunks into vector representations.

5. Vector database
Store embeddings and metadata for semantic search.

6. Hybrid retrieval
Use both vector search and keyword/BM25 search to improve retrieval.

7. Reranking
Rerank retrieved chunks so the most useful evidence appears first.

8. Answer generation
Use an LLM to answer only from retrieved context.

9. Citations
Show source document name, section, page/paragraph if possible, and retrieved text.

10. Query classification
Classify query/ticket type into categories such as grievance, KYC, card issue, fees, refund, fraud, account issue, transfer issue, unsupported.

11. Risk flag
Flag low/medium/high risk depending on complaint type, deadline, money loss, fraud terms, or unsupported answer.

12. Draft response
Generate a human-reviewable response for a customer support agent.

13. Audit log
Store prompt, retrieved sources, answer, risk label, model, timestamp, latency, and user action.

14. Evaluation dashboard or report
Show evaluation scores on a 40-50 question test set.

15. Live deployment
Deploy app and API so reviewers, mentors, and potential stakeholders can test it.

Do not overbuild:
Do not try to build a full bank system, real authentication, real customer database, or live payment integrations. That would be too risky and too large.

8. IMPORTANT AI/ML CONCEPTS USED

8.1 Generative AI

Generative AI is used to generate natural-language answers, explanations, summaries, and draft support responses.

In this project, the LLM should not answer freely from memory. It should answer using retrieved source context. This is important because finance/compliance questions require correctness and traceability.

Possible LLM options:

- Gemini API
- Groq-hosted Llama models
- OpenAI API if available
- Anthropic Claude API if available
- Local small models only if hardware allows

8.2 RAG: Retrieval-Augmented Generation

RAG combines retrieval and generation.

Flow:

1. User asks question
2. System searches trusted document database
3. Relevant chunks are retrieved
4. LLM receives question plus retrieved chunks
5. LLM generates answer grounded in those chunks
6. System displays answer with citations

Why RAG is useful:

- Reduces hallucination
- Uses current/domain-specific documents
- Gives source-backed answers
- Allows domain knowledge without fine-tuning
- Makes answers more auditable

8.3 Vector database

A vector database stores embeddings. An embedding is a numeric representation of text meaning.

Example:
"credit card complaint not resolved" and "grievance pending for 30 days" may not use exactly the same words, but their meanings are related. Vector search can retrieve similar text based on meaning.

Possible vector database choices:

Beginner-friendly:
- ChromaDB
- FAISS

More production-like:
- Qdrant
- PostgreSQL + pgvector

Recommended:
Start with ChromaDB for MVP. If time permits, move to Qdrant or pgvector to look more production-ready.

8.4 Hybrid search

Vector search is good for semantic meaning. Keyword search is good for exact terms like "KYC", "Ombudsman", "30 days", "chargeback", "AML".

Hybrid search combines both:

- Dense retrieval: embeddings/vector similarity
- Sparse retrieval: BM25/keyword search

Why it matters:
In finance and compliance, exact words and legal terms matter. Hybrid search can retrieve better evidence than vector search alone.

8.5 Reranking

Initial retrieval may return 10-20 chunks. A reranker scores which chunks are most relevant to the exact question.

Possible reranker:
- sentence-transformers cross-encoder
- Cohere rerank if API available
- BGE reranker if local setup works

Why it matters:
Reranking often improves answer quality by putting the best evidence at the top.

8.6 Classification

The system can classify user tickets into categories:

- Grievance redressal
- Credit card fees
- Fraud or unauthorized transaction
- KYC/identity
- Refund/chargeback
- Account issue
- Transfer issue
- Unsupported/out-of-scope

Implementation options:

1. Rule-based classifier for MVP
2. Zero-shot LLM classifier
3. Fine-tuned/supervised classifier using BANKING77 or CFPB complaint data

Recommended:
Start with rules + LLM classifier. Add a simple scikit-learn classifier if time permits.

8.7 Evaluation

Evaluation is what separates this project from a basic demo.

Metrics to track:

- Retrieval precision: Are retrieved chunks relevant?
- Context recall: Did retrieval find the needed information?
- Faithfulness: Is the answer supported by retrieved context?
- Answer relevancy: Does the answer address the question?
- Latency: How long does the answer take?
- Unsupported-answer rate: How often does it refuse when sources are insufficient?

Tools:

- RAGAS
- DeepEval
- Manual evaluation spreadsheet
- pytest tests for fixed examples

Minimum evaluation:
Create 40-50 test questions with expected source documents and run before/after comparisons:

- Baseline: vector search only
- Improved version: hybrid search + reranking

This allows a concrete product-validation claim:
"Hybrid retrieval plus reranking improved source selection over vector-only retrieval on a 50-question finance-policy evaluation set."


9. DATA SOURCES

Use only public and legally usable data. Do not scrape private customer data or confidential documents.

9.1 Source strategy

The source corpus should be divided into four layers:

Layer 1: Authoritative regulatory sources
These should be treated as the highest-trust sources for answers. Examples:

- RBI credit/debit card issuance and conduct directions
- RBI KYC / AML / CFT directions
- RBI Integrated Ombudsman Scheme and FAQs
- RBI customer-service and grievance-redressal circulars
- RBI customer protection circulars for unauthorized electronic banking transactions
- RBI failed-transaction TAT and compensation circulars
- RBI Internal Ombudsman directions
- RBI digital lending directions
- RBI credit information company grievance/compensation directions
- RBI Charter of Customer Rights / customer-rights review material
- RBI PPI / wallet unauthorized transaction liability framework
- RBI recovery-agent / collection-harassment conduct rules
- RBI payment aggregator dispute management and refund rules
- RBI Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) system for digital payments
- RBI digital payment security / tokenisation / account-aggregator consent references
- RBI Ombudsman annual reports for complaint category taxonomy
- RBI deposit account operation and service-charge directions
- RBI cheque collection and delayed clearing compensation guidance
- RBI RTGS / NEFT / IMPS dispute and compensation FAQs
- RBI safe deposit locker directions and deceased customer claim settlement directions
- Public bank customer compensation policies and depositor rights policies
- RBI loan penal charges, Key Facts Statement, floating-rate EMI reset and fair-lending sources
- RBI BBPS, NACH, AePS and other payment-system dispute sources
- RBI UDGAM / unclaimed deposit and DICGC deposit insurance sources
- RBI senior citizen, differently-abled and visually impaired banking facility sources
- RBI fraud-awareness and Sachet unauthorized-deposit reporting sources
- RBI loan penal charges, floating-rate reset, BBPS, NACH, AePS, UDGAM, DICGC, accessibility, and fraud-awareness sources should be treated as advanced banking modules after the core card/UPI/Ombudsman MVP.

Layer 2: Official bank / regulated-entity public documents
These should be used for institution-specific workflows, not as universal rules. Examples:

- HDFC, Axis, ICICI, SBI Card, Kotak, IDFC FIRST, IndusInd, RBL, YES Bank, and Standard Chartered public credit-card MITC documents
- Public grievance redressal policies
- Public compensation frameworks
- Public support/escalation pages
- Public nodal officer / principal nodal officer escalation material

Layer 3: Public product/network documents
These support UPI/payment use cases. Examples:

- NPCI UPI Help guidelines
- NPCI UPI Help Assistant pilot documents
- NPCI chargeback / UDIR / dispute redressal references
- Official UPI circulars where downloadable

Layer 4: Dataset and research references
These are not final-answer policy sources. Use them for classification, evaluation, product design, and architecture:

- BANKING77 for banking intent classification
- CFPB Consumer Complaint Database for complaint taxonomy and public complaint examples
- RAGAS / Langfuse references for evaluation
- Enterprise AI architecture references from AWS, Stripe, American Express, Dataiku, etc.

Layer 5: Insurance expansion sources
Insurance is a valid adjacent problem area, but it should be implemented as a separate module or second index, not mixed blindly into the first banking/UPI corpus. Insurance has different regulators, complaint routes, claim documents, timelines, exclusions, and policy wording conventions.

Useful insurance source categories:

- IRDAI policyholder protection and grievance redressal regulations
- IRDAI master circulars on health insurance business
- Insurance Ombudsman Rules and FAQs
- Bima Bharosa / IRDAI grievance complaint process
- Health insurance cashless and reimbursement claim documents
- Customer Information Sheet (CIS) and standard exclusions in health insurance
- Arogya Sanjeevani standard health insurance policy examples
- Saral Jeevan Bima standard term life insurance policy examples
- Motor insurance claim guidance, own damage, third party, theft claims
- Bharat Griha Raksha / home insurance standard wordings

Recommended insurance submodules:

1. Health insurance claim assistant
Use for: cashless vs reimbursement, network hospital, TPA, claim documents, exclusions, pre-existing disease waiting periods, OPD claim limitations, claim rejection explanation.

2. Term/life insurance claim assistant
Use for: death claim documents, nominee/assignee, death certificate, original policy, waiting period, accidental vs non-accidental death in standard term products.

3. Motor/car insurance claim assistant
Use for: own damage, third-party claim, theft claim, FIR/police intimation, RC copy, repair estimate, cashless garage, non-traceable certificate.

4. Property/home insurance claim assistant
Use for: fire/flood/theft/property damage, immediate intimation, surveyor, proof of loss, claim form, Bharat Griha Raksha coverage and exclusions.

5. Insurance grievance assistant
Use for: insurer GRO, IRDAI/Bima Bharosa, Insurance Ombudsman, one-month insurer response condition, claim delay, claim repudiation, policy servicing complaint.

Scope warning:
If insurance is added, the product should become a broader "Financial Services Trust Copilot" rather than only a banking compliance copilot. For the first MVP, either keep insurance out, or build it as a separate tab/index called "Insurance Claims". Do not let banking RBI rules answer insurance IRDAI questions.

12. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

High-level flow:

1. Admin loads trusted documents
2. System extracts text
3. Text is cleaned and split into chunks
4. Chunks are embedded
5. Embeddings are stored in vector DB with metadata
6. User asks question or pastes ticket
7. Query classifier detects category and risk
8. Retriever searches vector DB + BM25 index
9. Reranker orders the best chunks
10. LLM generates grounded answer using only retrieved chunks
11. System displays answer, citations, source chunks, risk label, and draft response
12. Audit logger stores request, sources, answer, model, latency, and result
13. Evaluation module tests answer quality on a fixed dataset

18. IMPORTANT QUESTIONS SENIORS/PROFESSORS MAY ASK

Question 1:
Is this project actually useful?

Answer:
Yes, as a prototype of a real enterprise pattern. Finance and compliance teams need source-grounded, auditable assistants for regulatory questions, customer support, and policy lookup. Our version is not production-ready but demonstrates the architecture and engineering principles.

Question 2:
Why not just use ChatGPT?

Answer:
ChatGPT alone may answer from general memory and may hallucinate. FinTrust AI retrieves from trusted documents, cites sources, logs the interaction, and refuses unsupported questions.

Question 3:
Why RAG instead of fine-tuning?

Answer:
RAG is better for document-grounded questions where sources change and citations are required. Fine-tuning may teach style or domain patterns, but it does not guarantee source-specific answers or current document grounding.

Question 4:
What is the ML part?

Answer:
The ML/AI parts include embeddings, semantic search, intent classification, reranking, LLM-based generation, evaluation metrics, and possibly supervised classification using BANKING77/CFPB data.

Question 5:
What is the backend part?

Answer:
FastAPI serves query, document ingestion, retrieval, answer generation, classification, audit logging, and evaluation endpoints.

Question 6:
How will you prove it works?

Answer:
We will create a 40-50 question evaluation set and compare vector-only retrieval against hybrid retrieval with reranking. We will measure faithfulness, answer relevance, retrieval precision, and latency.

Question 7:
What are the limitations?

Answer:
It uses public documents only, cannot provide legal/financial advice, may miss information if documents are incomplete, and requires human review for real-world use. It is an MVP prototype, not a production bank system.

Question 8:
What makes it different from a PDF chatbot?

Answer:
It includes domain-specific workflow, classification, risk labels, citations, audit logs, retrieval evaluation, human-review draft responses, and deployment.

Question 9:
What are the ethical concerns?

Answer:
The system must avoid final financial/legal advice, must not use private customer data, must make uncertainty visible, must cite sources, and must keep humans in control.

Question 10:
Can this be used by companies?

Answer:
The concept can be used by companies, but a real deployment would require security, access control, data governance, privacy review, model governance, monitoring, legal approval, and integration with internal systems.


19. RISKS AND HOW TO HANDLE THEM

Risk 1: It becomes just another chatbot

Solution:
Add citations, audit logs, classification, evaluation, and deployment.

Risk 2: Scope becomes too large

Solution:
Limit domain to credit card grievances, KYC, and support complaints for MVP.

Risk 3: Documents are messy

Solution:
Start with 10-20 clean public documents. Add more later.

Risk 4: RAG answers are poor

Solution:
Tune chunk size, add hybrid search, add reranking, improve prompt, evaluate systematically.

Risk 5: AI gives unsupported advice

Solution:
Add strict prompt, source requirement, refusal mode, and human review disclaimer.

Risk 6: Deployment fails

Solution:
Keep local demo ready, record video, use Streamlit Cloud or Hugging Face Spaces first.

Risk 7: Too many tools

Solution:
Start simple: FastAPI + Streamlit + ChromaDB + Gemini/Groq + SQLite. Add advanced tools only after MVP works.
